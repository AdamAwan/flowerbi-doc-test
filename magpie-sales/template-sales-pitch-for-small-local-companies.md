# Template Sales Pitch for Small Local Companies

A clear, repeatable sales pitch helps small local companies communicate their value quickly and consistently. This template is built for in-person networking, cold calls, and website “About” pages. It follows a **problem → solution → proof → close** structure that works across industries (bakeries, plumbers, consultants, retail shops, etc.).

> **Live template:** Replace bracketed `[placeholders]` with your own details. Keep the whole pitch under 60 seconds when spoken aloud.

---

## The Four-Part Pitch

### 1. Hook – The problem you solve

> “Most [small businesses in our area] struggle with [common pain point].”

*Example:* “Most independent coffee shops in Portland struggle to get repeat lunch traffic because they rely on generic loyalty cards.”

**Why it works:** Local customers recognize their own frustration. Naming the place (town, neighbourhood) builds instant relevance.

### 2. Offer – What you do differently

> “We help [customer type] [achieve desired outcome] by [unique method].”

*Example:* “We help coffee shops turn occasional visitors into regulars by offering a subscription-based rewards app that suggests personalised drink combos.”

**Keep it concrete:** avoid jargon. Say “we fix your leaky roof in one visit” not “we provide integrated roofing solutions”.

### 3. Proof – Social evidence or track record

> “Over the last [timeframe], we’ve helped [number] customers [result]. For instance, [one customer story].”

*Example:* “Over the last two years, we’ve helped 40+ shops boost repeat visits by 35%. Baker’s Brew, for example, saw a 40% increase in lunch traffic within three months.”

**Tip:** If you’re just starting out, skip the number and use a personal credential: “I’ve spent 10 years managing local marketing campaigns for neighbourhood businesses.”

### 4. Call to Action – Next step

> “Let’s [low‑friction action] to see if this could work for you.”

*Example:* “Let’s grab a 15‑minute coffee and look at your current loyalty data—no commitment.”

**Good CTAs:** “Schedule a free 10‑minute call”, “Visit our website to claim a free consultation”, “Stop by our shop this Friday for a demo”.

## Full Pitch Example (60 seconds)

> “Hi, I’m Sarah from WrapStar. Most small gift shops in Bristol struggle to keep customers after the holiday rush because they rely on seasonal foot traffic. We help those shops turn occasional buyers into loyal year‑round customers by providing eco‑friendly wrapping services paired with a simple punch‑card system. In the last year, we’ve worked with 15 local boutiques, and The Green Gift saw a 25% increase in spring sales. I’d love to buy you a coffee this week and show you how it works—are you free Wednesday morning?”

## Adapting for Different Channels

| Channel | Changes |
|---------|---------|
| **In‑person (networking event)** | Use the full 60‑second version. Shake hands, make eye contact, pause after the hook. |
| **Cold call (phone)** | Shrink hook + offer to one sentence. Ask a permission question: “Is now a bad time?” Then deliver the pitch. |
| **Website / Google Business Profile** | Write the pitch in paragraph form under a “What We Do” heading. Add a link to a free e‑book or consultation form. |
| **Social media bio (Instagram, LinkedIn)** | Use the offer sentence only. Example: “Helping local bookshops in Brighton sell more with custom reading subscriptions. DM for a free trial.” |

## Customisation Checklist

Before using the pitch, tick each box:

- [ ] I have named a specific local place (town, neighbourhood, city)
- [ ] The problem is one my target customer actually complains about
- [ ] The offer is specific enough that a stranger understands what I do
- [ ] I have a real example or a credible credential ready
- [ ] The call to action requires almost no effort from the prospect
- [ ] I’ve practised the spoken version out loud three times (timed < 60 sec)

---

## When to Switch to a Longer Conversation

If the prospect says “Tell me more”, drop the template and ask **discovery questions**:

- “What’s your biggest challenge with [area] right now?”
- “Have you tried solving this before? What happened?”
- “What would a successful outcome look like for you?”

Use their answers to tailor the rest of the discussion. The template is only the opener; the real sale happens when you listen.
